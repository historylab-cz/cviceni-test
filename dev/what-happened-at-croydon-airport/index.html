A man being carried out in the photograph was Oskar Goldberg. In the family archive of his brother Norbert a number of materials about his case have remained well preserved.

Important information

Memories of Oskar’s brother Norbert

“My father was an important businessman and after the arrival of Germans he was advised not to stay at home, because Germans may look for him. [...] So he decided to leave immediately. […] As it was Friday [the eve of Sabbath, during which devout Jews may not travel], we had to go on foot, we were orthodox.

The only things my father took with him were a tallit and a tefillin. That was all that was left of the property of the rich, prosperous and respected businessman. We decided to go to Bohumín where my mother’s brother, Jakob Bronner, lived. […] from Bohumín we continued to my father’s birthplace, Auschwitz.

When Germans marched into Czechoslovakia, my brother Oskar, ten years older than me, was on a business trip in Poland. He worked for our textile company, travelled in the country and sold our products. […] As soon as German soldiers entered Ostrava, we called him and told him not to come back. Oskar had enough money and so he spoke with a few friends and organized a plane that they flew in from Warsaw to London. Unfortunalely they were denied a permit to enter. […] So my brother and his friends decided to apply for the status of refugees, but the British did not want to accept it and decided to send them back to Poland. There were ten Czech Jewish refugees, ten young men from Czechoslovakia. My brother was young but very resourceful and he played a crucial role in the eventual staying of the ten refugees. When they were to board the plane at the airport to be returned, my brother suddenly caused a big confusion. He screamed and shouted, lay down on the floor, kicked around him and refused to get up. Later he told us about it. There were several reporters who photographed him.

[...] The Danish pilot fortunately refused to take off. When he saw the confusion, he refused to take these desperate people aboard. It was the only plane from London to Warsaw. Again they had mazel [they were lucky]. So the ten men were awaiting their destiny while the British were deciding about them. Then an article was published on the front page of the Daily Mail after this event, with the picture of my brother being carried back into the plane. […] It caused a big outrage. […] In the end, all these ten were released and after a few days they got a permit to stay in England.”

The memories were compiled from interviews with Norbert Goldberg (recorded by David Lawson, edited by Lukáš Přibyl). Source: The Jewish Museum in Prague.

Letter to Oskar Goldberg from the family from the Protectorate. 9 May 1939

“It is really sad that you were left there only after such difficulties and such a comedy, but still I had to laugh heartily after reading your description and seeing the enclosed pictures. In any case you tried very much and you have become a heavyweight, Max thinks that on the basis of that you should go to Hollywood [!] After all even Frida sent us a similar cutting from a French newspaper in which you are being carried out.”

A letter to Oskar Goldberg from the family from the Protectorate, 9 May 1939. Source: The Jewish Museum in Prague.

Important information

Czech refugees who arrived will stay.

From our London correspondents London, Friday.

The adventure of eleven refugees of the Czech nationality, who reached Croydon on Wednesday and were detained there and who were to be returned by ship to Denmark yesterday, had a happy ending. The Jewish Aid Committee for refugees from Germany, which had not accepted responsibility for refugees from Czechoslovakia until recently, decided today to provide the necessary guarantees for them as an exceptional measure so that they could be accepted in the country.

We are learning from the authorities that this group arrived from Krakow in Poland in a rented plane, with a stopover in Copenhagen. […]

Local border guards [in Croydon] consulted the Ministry of the Interior that turned to refugee committees which did not know anything about this group and were not ready to sponsor them. As a result, these eleven people were detained and they were to be sent back by ship to Copenhagen.

It may be assumed that this group made a desperate attempt to enter our country due to the new visa system for Czechs arriving in England, which should take effect on 1 April and which was announced in January. […]

There are lots of stories circulating about the number of refugees returned during this week. I acquired these numbers. The total number of foreigners of all categories arriving in Croydon on Wednesday was 257, of which 118 were Germans and 45 were Czechs. 10 were rejected, all of them Czechs. 241 foreigners landed in Croydon yesterday, of whom 82 were Germans and 81 were Czechs. 20 Czechs were rejected entry.

Source: Manchester Guardian, 1 April 1939 (translated by Michal Frankl).
